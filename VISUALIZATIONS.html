Mixture of Bernoulli Models: EM vs Gradient-Based Optimization

🔬 Experimental Methodology

  • Dataset 1 (Synthetic): 500 samples from known 3-component mixture on 9 features. Used for controlled analysis of convergence behavior.
  • Dataset 2 (MNIST): 4,000 binarized training images (28×28 = 784 features). 10 components for digit clustering. Tests model on real-world image data.
  • Optimization Methods: EM (closed-form baseline), Vanilla GD, SGD, Momentum GD, Adam. All trained for identical iteration budgets.
  • Hyperparameters: Grid search over learning rates [0.001, 0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5]. 600 iterations per configuration on synthetic data.
  • Evaluation Metrics: Log-likelihood (quality), ARI (clustering accuracy), convergence speed (iterations to threshold), stability (variance across runs).

Convergence Trajectories

Key Finding: EM shows monotonic increase (theoretical guarantee). Adam reaches 99% of EM's final log-likelihood in ~35 iterations while EM takes ~120. This demonstrates the trade-off: EM is theoretically optimal for this model but GD methods converge faster in practice.

Learning Rate Sensitivity

Insight: Adam shows robustness to learning rate choice (flat performance across rates). SGD and Momentum are more sensitive. This explains Adam's popularity in practice: less hyperparameter tuning needed.

Time to Threshold (99% LL)

Finding: Adam reaches near-optimal performance fastest (~35 iters). EM monotonic but slower onset. SGD never reliably reaches threshold within 300 iterations.

🎯 Key Insights & Conclusions

1. EM Optimality: For the Mixture of Bernoulli model, EM achieves the best final log-likelihood and fastest convergence per iteration (superlinear rate). Monotonic likelihood increase is guaranteed.

2. Adam Practicality: Adam reaches 99% of EM's performance in 35 iterations vs EM's 120 iterations. More robust to learning rate choice. Better for large-scale/mini-batch learning.

3. Gradient Method Advantages: Gradient-based methods naturally extend to complex models (neural networks, regularization, priors). EM requires re-deriving M-step for each model variation.

4. Numerical Stability Crucial: Log-space computation prevents underflow. Epsilon clipping and softmax re-parametrization essential for high-dimensional data (784 features).

5. Model Limitations: Bernoulli mixture assumes pixel independence, producing blurry learned digits. Modern generative models (VAE, Diffusion, GAN) achieve better results by modeling dependencies.

6. Practical Takeaway: Choose EM for pure likelihood maximization on known exponential family models. Choose gradient methods (especially Adam) for flexibility, scalability, and extensibility.
